About

Most breakthrough science today is bottlenecked not by ideas, but by whether the code can keep up with the hardware. That gap is my whole career.

I lead the Computational Research and Programming Lab at the University of Delaware, where we build the software layer that lets domain scientists actually use GPU-accelerated and exascale machines — without becoming HPC experts themselves.

That work spans directive-based programming models (I co-authored the book on OpenACC and sit on its Board of Directors), compiler validation at exascale, and increasingly the use of LLMs to test and build scientific software. As Director of the First State AI Institute and Vice Chair of Delaware's AI Commission, I also help shape how an entire state prepares for an AI-driven future.

What we build

Five research thrusts, one throughline

From understanding raw hardware, to proving compilers correct, to enabling new science — the software my group creates is meant to outlast any single project.

IPDPS 2026
01

GPU & hardware characterization

Execution-centric microbenchmarking that dissects the newest accelerators — NVIDIA Blackwell's tensor cores and FP4/FP6 precision, AMD MI300A's FP8 and structured sparsity — so developers know what the silicon actually does.

Blackwell B200MI300Amicrobenchmarks
70+ citations
02

Validation & verification

The OpenMP and OpenACC test suites that keep NVIDIA, Cray, LLVM, Intel and GNU compilers honest across Summit, Frontier, Perlmutter and Aurora. LLM4VV extends this — putting LLMs to work as autonomous test generators and judges.

SOLLVEOpenMP_VVLLM4VV
03

ML for biomedicine

With the National Cancer Institute and Nemours Children's Health, we built UNNT and RCDML — open frameworks for drug-response prediction. One reaches ~90% AUC predicting therapy response at AML diagnosis, with biologically interpretable gene signatures.

NCI / NIHrare diseaseprecision oncology
Best Paper Nom.
04

Refactoring code for faster science

Taking flagship simulations to the GPU: the MURaM solar model, OpenACC-accelerated PhysiCell cancer simulation (9.3 hrs → 1.7 hrs), and "The Artificial Scientist," which learns from plasma simulations in-transit, bypassing the filesystem entirely.

05

DARSE — democratizing research software engineering

A national gap: too few engineers who can work fluently across AI, scientific computing, and domain science. DARSE embeds trained research software engineers alongside scientists on long-horizon problems — ~24 active projects across nine domains, building a workforce pipeline for Delaware and the Mid-Atlantic.
